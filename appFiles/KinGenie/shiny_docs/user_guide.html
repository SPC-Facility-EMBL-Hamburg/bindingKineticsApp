KinGenie allows the processing, analysis and visualization of binding kinetics data.

Input files

Octet Biolayer Interferometry

Input one or many files with the '.frd' extension. These are JSON formatted files. Moreover, you can load the Method file with extension '.fmf'. All files corresponding to the same experiment should be selected together for loading the data. A complete example folder can be downloaded here.



Gator Biolayer Interferometry

Input the csv files with the traces data ('Assay_#_Channel#.csv') and the corresponding files describing the metadata (Setting.ini and ExperimentStep.ini). All files corresponding to the same experiment should be selected together for loading the data.



KinGenie simulation csv

If you performed a simulation for surface-based binding, you can export the results and import the csv file into KinGenie.


Processing kinetic traces

There are several options for processing the kinetics traces. If the operations are performed 'in-place', then the sensor traces will be modified. Otherwise, the sensor traces will remain unchanged, and the new traces will be generated.

  • Align the association phases: For each association step, KinGenie finds the previous step, computes the mean of the last ten points, and subtracts this value from the next steps.
  • Subtract baseline: One trace is selected and subtracted from other traces.
  • Average: The selected traces are averaged.
  • Inter-step correction (dissociation): For each dissociation step, KinGenie finds the previous association step and computes the mean of the last n points (from one to ten). Then, the difference between this value and the mean of the first n points (from one to ten) is subtracted from the dissociation step.
  • Subtract experiment: Given two different experiments with an equal number of sensors, equal number of steps and time step, the signal data from one experiment is subtracted from the signal data from the other experiment, sensor by sensor.

A common workflow would be to load the data, align the association phases, subtract the baseline, and then go to the analysis section. The inter-step correction is only necessary if there are big jumps due to buffer mismatch. For fast off rates, it is not recommended because data may be lost.

Surface-based binding

In this case, one of the interacting partners is attached to a surface (e.g., a sensor chip), and the other partner flows over the surface at a constant concentration.


Planning an experiment

Appropriate controls are essential to obtain useful data. Find below a list of recommendations based on David G. Myszka (1999).

1. Blank injection: Inject buffer alone over a surface with the ligand to identify signal artifacts from the injection process. The signal from the blank injection should be subtracted from the analyte signal.

2. Reference surface: Inject the analyte (at the highest concentration) over a surface without the ligand (or with a non-binding protein) to control for non-specific binding. Ideally, no signal change should be observed. To perform double-reference subtraction, this control should be performed for each analyte concentration.

3. Analyte concentrations: Use a series of at least 5â€“7 analyte concentrations that span tenfold above and below the expected \( K_{\text{d}} \).

4. Mass transport limitation (MTL): Run the same analyte at different flow rates or ligand loading levels to verify that binding is not limited by diffusion. To minimize MTL effects, use a high flow rate and the lowest possible loading level that still provides a good signal-to-noise ratio.

5. Ligand activity: Confirm that the immobilized ligand is active and correctly captured by injecting a known positive control analyte.

6. Use oriented immobilization: Avoid random coupling procedures, such as those involving primary amines, which introduce surface heterogeneity. Prioritize capturing methods such as antibodies or fusion tags, that create homogeneous surfaces.


One to one

The chemical equation of the system is:

$$ \large{ A + B \rightleftharpoons AB } $$

where \( A \) is the attached molecule, \( B \) is the molecule flowing and \( AB \) is the complex. The signal \( S \) is proportional to the concentration of the complex, and follows the differential equation:

$$ \large{ \frac{dS}{dt} = \frac{k_{\text{off}}}{K_{\text{d}}} [B] (S_{\text{max}} - S) - k_{\text{off}} S } $$

where \( k_{\text{off}} \) is the dissociation constant, \( K_{\text{d}} \) is the equilibrium dissociation constant, and \( S_{\text{max}} \) is the maximum signal (at infinite concentration of \( B \)). The association rate constant \( k_{\text{on}} \) is given by:

$$ \large{ k_{\text{on}} = \frac{k_{\text{off}}}{K_{\text{d}}} } $$

During the dissociation step, the concentration of \( B \) is zero, simplifying the signal equation to:

$$ \large{ \frac{dS}{dt} = - k_{\text{off}} S } $$

References:

  • Schuck, P., & Zhao, H. (2010). The role of mass transport limitation and surface heterogeneity in the biophysical characterization of macromolecular binding processes by SPR biosensing. Surface plasmon resonance: methods and protocols, 15-54.


One to one (mass transport limitation)

The chemical equations of the system are:

$$ \large{ B_{\text{bulk}} \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{tr}}}_{k_{\text{tr}}}} B_{\text{surface}} } $$ $$ \large{ B_{\text{surface}} + A \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{on}}}_{k_{\text{off}}}} AB } $$

where \( A \) is the attached molecule, \( B \) is the molecule flowing and \( AB \) is the complex. The signal \( S \) is proportional to the concentration of the complex, and follows the differential equation:

$$ \large{ \frac{dS}{dt} = \frac{k_{\text{off}}}{K_{\text{d}}} \cdot [B_{\text{surface}}] \cdot (S_{\text{max}} - S) - k_{\text{off}} \cdot S } $$

with

$$ \large{ \frac{d [B_{\text{surface}}]}{dt} = k_{\text{tr}} \cdot ([B]_{\text{0}} - [B_{\text{surface}}]) - \frac{dS}{dt} } $$

where \( k_{\text{off}} \) is the dissociation constant, \( K_{\text{d}} \) is the equilibrium dissociation constant, and \( S_{\text{max}} \) is the maximum signal (at infinite concentration of \( B \)). The association rate constant \( k_{\text{on}} \) is given by:

$$ \large{ k_{\text{on}} = \frac{k_{\text{off}}}{K_{\text{d}}} } $$

During the dissociation step, the concentration of \( B \) is zero, simplifying the signal equation to:

$$ \large{ \frac{dS(t)}{dt} = \frac{-k_{\text{off}} S(t)}{1 + \frac{k_{\text{on}}}{k_{\text{tr}}} \left( S_{\max} - S(t) \right)} } $$

References:

  • Schuck, P., & Zhao, H. (2010). The role of mass transport limitation and surface heterogeneity in the biophysical characterization of macromolecular binding processes by SPR biosensing. Surface plasmon resonance: methods and protocols, 15-54.


One to one (induced fit)

The chemical equations of the system are:

$$ \large{ A + B \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{on}}}_{k_{\text{off}}}} AB_{\text{int}} } $$ $$ \large{ AB_{\text{int}} \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{c}}}_{k_{\text{rev}}}} AB } $$

where \( A \) is the attached molecule, \( B \) is the molecule flowing, \( AB_{\text{int}} \) is the intermediate complex, and \( AB \) is the induced complex. Regarding the signal, the set of non-redundant differential equations is:

$$ \begin{aligned} -\frac{dS}{dt} &= (- k_{\text{off}} - k_{\text{on}} [B]) S - k_{\text{off}} S_1 \\ \end{aligned} $$ $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dS_1}{dt} &= (- k_\text{c}) S + (- k_\text{c} - k_{\text{rev}}) S_1 \end{aligned} $$

where \( S \) is the total signal, and \( S_{\text{1}} \) is the signal produced by the induced complex only. During the dissociation step, the concentration of \( B \) is zero, simplifying the differential equations to:

$$ \begin{aligned} -\frac{dS}{dt} &= (- k_{\text{off}}) S - k_{\text{off}} S_1 \\ \end{aligned} $$ $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dS_1}{dt} &= (- k_\text{c}) S + (- k_\text{c} - k_{\text{rev}}) S_1 \end{aligned} $$

One to one (conformational selection)

The chemical equations of the system are:

$$ \large{ A_\text{inactive} \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{c}}}_{k_{\text{rev}}}} A_\text{active} } $$ $$ \large{ A_\text{active} + B \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{on}}}_{k_{\text{off}}}} AB } $$

where \( A_\text{inactive} \) is the attached molecule in the inactive state, \( A_\text{active} \) is the attached molecule in the active state (capable of binding \( B \)), \( B \) is the molecule flowing, and \( AB \) is the complex. Regarding the signal, the set of non-redundant differential equations is:

$$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dS_1}{dt} &= (- k_c - k_{\text{rev}}) S_1 - k_{\text{rev}} S \end{aligned} $$ $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dS}{dt} &= (- k_{\text{on}} [B]) S_1 + (- k_{\text{on}} [B] - k_{\text{off}}) S \end{aligned} $$

where \( S \) is the total signal, and \( S_{\text{1}} \) is some value proportional to the amount of \( A_{\text{inactive}} \). During the dissociation step, the concentration of \( B \) is zero, simplifying the differential equations to:

$$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dS_1}{dt} &= (- k_c - k_{\text{rev}}) S_1 - k_{\text{rev}} S \end{aligned} $$ $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dS}{dt} &= - k_{\text{off}} S \end{aligned} $$
Solution-based binding

In this case, the interacting species are in solution and the concentrations of all species change over time.

One to one

The chemical equation of the system is:

$$ \large{ A + B \rightleftharpoons AB } $$

where \( A \) and \( B \) are the two interacting partners, and \( AB \) is the complex. The concentration of the complex follows the differential equation:

$$ \large{ \frac{d[\text{AB}]}{dt} = k_{\text{on}} [A] [B] - k_{\text{off}} [\text{AB}] } $$

where \( k_{\text{off}} \) is the dissociation constant, \( k_{\text{on}} \) is the association constant. The equilibrium dissociation constant \( K_{\text{d}} \) is given by:

$$ \large{ K_{\text{d}} = \frac{k_{\text{off}}}{k_{\text{on}}} } $$

One to one (induced fit)

The chemical equations of the system are:

$$ \large{ A + B \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{on}}}_{k_{\text{off}}}} AB_{\text{int}} } $$ $$ \large{ AB_{\text{int}} \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{c}}}_{k_{\text{rev}}}} AB } $$

where \( A \) and \( B \) are the interacting molecules, \( AB_{\text{int}} \) is the intermediate complex, and \( AB \) is the induced complex. The set of non-redundant differential equations is:

$$ \large{ \frac{d[B]}{dt} = k_{\text{off}} ([B]_{\text{0}} - [B] - [AB]) - k_{\text{on}} ([B] - [B]_{\text{0}} + [A]_{\text{0}})[B] } $$ $$ \large{ \frac{d[AB]}{dt} = k_{\text{c}} ([B]_{\text{0}} - [B] - [AB]) - k_{\text{rev}} [AB] } $$

where \( [B]_{\text{0}} \) and \( [A]_{\text{0}} \) are the total concentrations of \( B \) and \( A \), respectively.

$$ \large{ [B]_{\text{0}} = [B] + [AB] + [AB_{\text{int}}] } $$ $$ \large{ [A]_{\text{0}} = [A] + [AB] + [AB_{\text{int}}] } $$

The smallest dominant relaxation rate is given by:

$$ \large{ k_{\text{obs,1}} = k_{\text{c}} + k_{\text{rev}} + \frac{\gamma}{2} - \frac{\sqrt{\gamma^2 + 4 k_{\text{off}} k_{\text{rev}}}}{2} } $$

while the second relaxation rate is given by:

$$ \large{ k_{\text{obs,2}} = k_{\text{c}} + k_{\text{rev}} + \frac{\gamma}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{\gamma^2 + 4 k_{\text{off}} k_{\text{rev}}}}{2} } $$

where,

$$ \large{ \gamma = -k_{\text{c}} - k_{\text{rev}} + k_{\text{off}} + k_{\text{on}} (\delta - K_\text{D}^{\text{app}}) } $$ $$ \large{ \delta = \sqrt{(\text{[B]}_{\text{0}} - \text{[A]}_{\text{0}} + K_\text{D}^{\text{app}})^2 + 4 \text{[A]}_{\text{0}} K_\text{D}^{\text{app}}} } $$ $$ \large{ K_\text{D}^{\text{app}} = \frac{k_{\text{off}} k_{\text{c}}}{k_{\text{on}} (k_{\text{c}} + k_{\text{rev}})} } $$

References:

  • Paul, F., & Weikl, T. R. (2016). How to distinguish conformational selection and induced fit based on chemical relaxation rates. PLoS computational biology, 12(9), e1005067.


One to one (conformational selection)

The chemical equations of the system are:

$$ \large{ A_\text{inactive} \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{c}}}_{k_{\text{rev}}}} A_\text{active} } $$ $$ \large{ A_\text{active} + B \mathrel{\mathop{\rightleftharpoons}\limits^{k_{\text{on}}}_{k_{\text{off}}}} AB } $$

where \( A_\text{inactive} \) is the 'receptor' molecule in the inactive state, \( A_\text{active} \) is the 'receptor' molecule in the active state (capable of binding \( B \)), \( B \) is the ligand, and \( AB \) is the complex. The set of non-redundant differential equations is:

$$ \large{ \frac{d}{dt}[A_{\text{active}}] = k_{\text{c}} ([A]_{\text{0}} - [A_{\text{active}}]) - (k_{\text{rev}} + k_{\text{on}}[B])[A_{\text{active}}] + (k_{\text{off}} - k_{\text{c}})([B]_{\text{0}} - [B]) } $$ $$ \large{ \frac{d}{dt}[B] = k_{\text{off}} ([B]_{\text{0}} - [B]) - k_{\text{on}}[A_{\text{active}}][B] } $$

where \( [B]_{\text{0}} \) and \( [A]_{\text{0}} \) are the total concentrations of \( B \) and \( A \), respectively.

$$ \large{ [B]_{\text{0}} = [B] + [AB] } $$ $$ \large{ [A]_{\text{0}} = [A]_{\text{inactive}} + [A]_{\text{active}} + [AB] } $$

The smallest dominant relaxation rate is given by:

$$ \large{ k_{\text{obs,1}} = k_{\text{c}} + \frac{\alpha}{2} - \frac{\sqrt{\alpha ^2 + \beta}}{2} } $$

while the second relaxation rate is given by:

$$ \large{ k_{\text{obs,2}} = k_{\text{c}} + \frac{\alpha}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha ^2 + \beta}}{2}} $$

where,

$$ \large{ \alpha = k_{\text{rev}} - k_{\text{c}} } $$ $$ \large{ \delta = \sqrt{(\text{[B]}_{\text{0}} - \text{[A]}_{\text{0}} + K_\text{D}^{\text{app}})^2 + 4 \text{[A]}_{\text{0}} K_\text{D}^{\text{app}}} } $$ $$ \large{ K_\text{D}^{\text{app}} = \frac{k_{\text{off}} k_{\text{c}}}{k_{\text{on}} (k_{\text{c}} + k_{\text{rev}})} } $$

References:

  • Paul, F., & Weikl, T. R. (2016). How to distinguish conformational selection and induced fit based on chemical relaxation rates. PLoS computational biology, 12(9), e1005067.


Fitting

For now, KinGenie only supports surface-based binding models. The steady-state data can be analysed with the one-to-one model, while the kinetic data can be also analysed with the one-to-one mass transport limitation (MTL) model. Fitting the steady-state will provide the equilibrium dissociation constant \( K_{\text{d}} \). Fitting the kinetic data will provide the equilibrium dissociation constant \( K_{\text{d}} \) and the dissociation rate constant \( k_{\text{off}} \).

The fittings are based on numerical integration of the differential equations and the minimization of the sum of the squared residuals between the experimental data and the model predictions. When creating a new dataset for analysis, traces with the same sample ID are fitted with global parameters (e.g., \( K_{\text{d}} \) , \( k_{\text{off}} \)). If the option 'Smax Linked By Sensor' is activated when doing the fitting, the \( S_{\text{max}} \) parameter is shared between traces with the same sample ID and replicate number.

When fitting the steady-state data, the asymmetric confidence intervals are calculated automatically. When fitting the kinetic data (association and dissociation phases), the asymmetric confidence intervals are calculated by pressing on the button 'e. Confidence interval'. Please remember that low error values do not necessarily mean that the selected model is the one describing the underlying data generating process. For example, if the binding process follows a one-to-one with MTL model, the fitted parameters of a one-to-one model (without MTL) may have low errors, but be still far-off from the true values.


Observed constants

When fitting the association phase with the one-to-one binding model, a plot of the observed constants versus ligand concentration is provided. The observed constants are fitted independently for each trace, using a single exponential function:

$$ \large{ Y = a0 + a1*\exp(-k_{\text{obs}} t) } $$

In the ideal case, the observed constants should be linear with the ligand concentration.
